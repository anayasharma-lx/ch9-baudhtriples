The odd-square method
Choose any odd number m that is a perfect square.
It is the (n)th odd number where m = 2n − 1. Then: (n−1)² + m = n² Since m is a square, write m = k². So: (n−1)² + k² = n² This gives the triple (k, n−1, n).

This method produces triples where one smaller side is exactly 1 less than the hypotenuse. It does not generate all primitive triples — for example (8, 15, 17) cannot be made this way.
If (a, b, c) is a Baudhāyana triple, then (ka, kb, kc) is also a triple for any positive integer k. This is because (ka)²+(kb)²=k²(a²+b²)=k²c²=(kc)².
